{% raw %}CHAPTER IV{% endraw %}
{% raw %}THE GAME.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Next morning, however, the impressions of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}night became faint, though not to the point of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}obliteration. He found himself with all Paris before{% endraw %}
{% raw %}him and every opportunity of having a ' high old{% endraw %}
{% raw %}time of it,' to adopt the phrase of the vivacious{% endraw %}
{% raw %}schoolboy.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As he was about to start on his day's round of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amusement, one of his retainers entered with a note{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on a salver, saying in his Dravidian patois that the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mem sahib had sent a chit by a messenger with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plenty many salaams for the sahib.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}' Shiva and Shravanabellagoolah,' quoth Yussuff.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}' The chits commencing again. When will history{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stop repeating itself!'{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There could be no mistake as to the nature of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}neat missive, with its heavy odour of musk. Mrs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tacker told him where she would be that evening,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and promised to obtain for him a card for that{% endraw %}
{% raw %}function. He answered in a note, promising to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}there after supper. He went first to another place,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graced by the presence of Miss Browne. He told{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mrs. Tacker boldly that he could not live without{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a little gambling after dinner. She freely forgave{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his delay on this excuse. She was most agreeable,{% endraw %}
